Introduction

What is the Periodic Table?

Arrangement of all 118 elements by increasing atomic number, so elements with similar properties are grouped together.

What is Periodicity?

The repetition of properties of elements at regular intervals when arranged by atomic number.

Slide 04 / 16

Structure of the Periodic Table

↔ 7 Periods
  • • Horizontal rows
  • • Period number = no. of electron shells
  • • Period 1: 2 elements; Period 6: 32 elements
↕ 18 Groups
  • • Vertical columns
  • • Group = same valence electrons
  • • Groups 1–2 (s-block) + 13–18 (p-block)

📌 Electronic Configuration is the basis — same group = same valence electrons = similar chemical properties. Period number = number of occupied electron shells.

Slide 05 / 16

Types of Elements — s, p, d, f Blocks

s-block Groups 1–2
  • • Alkali metals (Group 1) & Alkaline earth metals (Group 2)
  • • Last electron enters s-orbital
  • • Highly reactive, form ionic compounds
p-block Groups 13–18
  • • Non-metals, metalloids, noble gases
  • • Last electron enters p-orbital
  • • Most diverse block in properties
d-block Groups 3–12
  • • Transition metals (Fe, Cu, Zn…)
  • • Variable oxidation states
  • • Good conductors, catalytic properties
f-block Inner Transition
  • • Lanthanoids (57–71) & Actinoids (89–103)
  • • Last electron enters f-orbital
  • • Placed separately at bottom of table

Electron Gain Enthalpy & Electronegativity

Electron Gain Enthalpy (EGA)

Energy change when a gaseous atom gains one electron

  • → Becomes more negative across a period
  • ↓ Becomes less negative down a group
  • ⚡ Cl has highest EGA (−349 kJ/mol)
  • 🚫 Noble gases — zero / positive EGA

Electronegativity (EN)

Tendency to attract a bonding pair of electrons (Pauling scale)

  • → Increases across a period
  • ↓ Decreases down a group
  • ⭐ F = 3.98 (highest of all elements)
  • 🥈 O = 3.44  |  N = 3.04  |  Cl = 3.16

Slide 10 / 16

Factors Affecting Periodicity

⚛️

Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)

Net positive charge experienced by outermost electrons. Zeff = Z − σ (shielding constant). Higher Zeff → smaller radius, higher IE.

🛡️

Shielding Effect

Inner shell electrons reduce the nuclear pull on outer electrons. More inner shells → greater shielding → lower Zeff felt by outermost electrons.

📏

Atomic Size

Larger atoms have loosely held outer electrons with greater distance from nucleus — lower IE, lower EN, less negative EGA.

🔗 Interconnection: Zeff increases across a period (same shell, more protons) → atomic radius decreases → IE and EN increase. Down a group, shielding increases → Zeff felt less → radius increases → IE decreases.

Slide 11 / 16

Anomalies in Periodic Trends

When orbital stability overrides the general periodic trend

⚠ Be > B (IE)

Beryllium (900) > Boron (801) kJ/mol

Be: 1s² [↑↓] ← fully filled 2s²
B: 1s² 2s² [↑] ← lone 2p electron

Fully-filled 2s² is extra stable — harder to remove

⚠ N > O (IE)

Nitrogen (1402) > Oxygen (1314) kJ/mol

N: 2p³ [↑][↑][↑] ← half-filled
O: 2p⁴ [↑↓][↑][↑] ← paired e⁻

Half-filled 2p³ is extra stable; O's paired e⁻ repel easier removal

⚠ Noble Gases (EGA)

Zero or positive electron gain enthalpy

He: 1s² [↑↓] fully filled
Ne: 2p⁶ [↑↓][↑↓][↑↓]

Complete octet — gaining an electron would be energetically unfavourable
